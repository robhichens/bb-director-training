## Two Types of Inspections

### 1. Announced Annual Inspection
Virginia VDSS licensing conducts at least one announced inspection per year. You'll typically receive a few weeks' notice. This is your primary opportunity to demonstrate full compliance and build a positive relationship with your licensing representative.

### 2. Unannounced Inspections
Licensing can and does show up without notice — especially in response to a complaint, a serious incident report, or as a routine check on centers with prior deficiencies. Treat every day as if an inspector might walk in at any time, because one might.

## What Inspectors Look At

Virginia inspectors work from the standards in **8VAC20-780** (Child Day Center Regulations). They are checking every area of your operation. The categories below are where most deficiencies originate.

### Staff Records and Ratios

This is the #1 citation area statewide.

**Ratios** — inspectors will walk each classroom and count children against staff:
- Infants (under 16 months): 1:4
- Toddlers (16–24 months): 1:5
- Two-year-olds: 1:8
- Preschool (3–4 year olds): 1:10
- School-age: 1:18

**Staff files** — inspectors will pull files. Each staff member's file must contain:
- Completed application
- References (checked and documented)
- Fingerprint/background check clearance
- TB screening (current within 2 years)
- Current CPR/First Aid certification
- Orientation hours completed
- Health statement
- Signed acknowledgment of policies

If any of these are missing or expired for **any** staff member, it's a deficiency.

### Child Records

Each enrolled child must have a current file containing:
- Enrollment application with emergency contacts
- Immunization records (current and complete)
- Physical/health form (within required timeframes)
- Authorization for emergency medical care
- Any special needs plans or medication authorizations

Inspectors will pull 3–5 child files at random. If a file is incomplete, that's a deficiency — and they may pull more.

### Health and Safety

- **Bleach solution** — correct concentration, mixed daily, labeled. If your bottles are undated or smell faint, it's a deficiency.
- **First aid kits** — fully stocked per requirements. FL: 3 kits, MC+CR: 2 kits. Inspectors will open and check contents.
- **Medicine box** — only inhalers and EpiPens. Anything else is a violation.
- **Diapering area** — paper liner used and discarded for each change, proper sanitization, log maintained
- **Nap** — infants on back only, no propping, individual cribs, whiteboard current
- **Emergency contacts and first aid kits** accessible in every classroom — not locked in an office

### Physical Environment

- **Square footage** — 35 sq ft per child indoors, 75 sq ft per child outdoors (measured usable space)
- **Bathroom ratios** — 1 toilet per 15 children
- **Lighting** — minimum foot-candles in classrooms and nap areas
- **Temperature** — 65–80°F maintained in occupied spaces
- **Hazardous materials** — locked and inaccessible to children. Cleaning supplies, bleach, medications — all locked.
- **Outdoor equipment** — no visible hazards, no broken equipment, fall zones maintained

### Program and Curriculum

- **Lesson plans** — on file, current, accessible. Inspectors will ask to see them.
- **Daily schedule** — posted in each classroom
- **Screen time** — if televisions/screens are present, usage logs may be requested
- **Classroom postings** — emergency information, parent rights notice, menu, attendance record visible

### Fire Safety and Emergency Preparedness

- **Fire drill log** — one drill per month, documented with date, time, evacuation time, and number of children
- **Fire extinguishers** — current inspection tags
- **Emergency evacuation plan** — posted in every room, shows primary and secondary exits
- **72-hour emergency kit** — complete and accessible (see Module 5 full contents list)
- **Emergency contact information** accessible in every classroom, not just the office

## The Inspection Day: What to Do

### When the Inspector Arrives

**Stay calm.** Inspectors notice how directors handle their arrival. A composed, professional greeting signals a well-run center.

> *"Good morning — I'm [name], the director. Welcome. Let me know what you need from me and I'll make sure you have it."*

Do not:
- Panic or look panicked
- Ask "can I call someone first?" (you can notify ownership, but the inspection proceeds)
- Ask them to wait while you "get things ready" — that's not an option with unannounced visits
- Follow them around the building hovering — it signals anxiety. Be available, not shadowing.

### During the Inspection

- **Do not coach or warn your teachers.** If you see an inspector heading toward a classroom, do not text the teacher. This can be perceived as preparation and undermines the inspection's integrity.
- **Answer questions honestly.** If a record is missing, say so. Do not guess or make up an answer.
- **Take notes.** Write down every question the inspector asks and every area they flag. You will need this for the exit conference.
- **Provide what's asked for promptly.** If a file takes more than 5 minutes to locate, that itself becomes a note about record-keeping.

### The Exit Conference

At the end of the inspection, the licensing representative will conduct an exit conference with you to review findings. This is your opportunity to:

- Ask for clarification on anything you don't understand
- Provide immediate context if something was temporarily out of compliance (e.g., "that staff member's TB test renewal is scheduled for Thursday — here's the appointment confirmation")
- Note anything that was corrected during the inspection itself

> Do not argue with findings at the exit conference. You can provide context and documentation, but the formal response process is the right channel for disputes — not the exit conference.

## Responding to a Violation

If you receive a violation (called a "deficiency" in Virginia), you must respond in writing within the timeframe specified — typically **10 business days** for most deficiencies, **immediately** for serious ones.

### Your Written Response Must Include

1. **Acknowledgment of the deficiency** — state the regulation cited
2. **Corrective action taken** — what you did to fix it and when
3. **Preventive measures** — what you're doing to make sure it doesn't happen again

### Example

> *Deficiency: Staff file for [employee] did not contain current TB screening.*
>
> *Corrective action: TB screening was completed on [date] and documentation has been placed in the staff file.*
>
> *Prevention: I have added a quarterly file audit to the first-of-month checklist (Day 3) to verify all staff records are current before they lapse.*

### Types of Violations

| Type | Description | Response Timeline |
|---|---|---|
| Non-serious deficiency | A standard compliance gap | 10 business days written response |
| Repeat deficiency | Same issue found in previous inspection | Faster response; may trigger follow-up visit |
| Serious deficiency | Immediate risk to child health or safety | Same-day corrective action, immediate written response |
| Mandatory closure | Conditions unsafe to operate | Center must close until corrected and approved |

Serious deficiencies and mandatory closures are rare — but they happen to centers that have been ignoring smaller deficiencies for a long time. The goal of ongoing compliance is to make sure you never face one.

## Year-Round Inspection Readiness

### Monthly (Day 3 — existing checklist)
- Pull 3 random child files and verify they're complete
- Pull 3 random staff files and verify all documents are current
- Check first aid kit contents
- Verify fire drill log is current
- Walk each classroom for posting compliance

### Quarterly
- Full staff file audit — every employee, every document, check expiration dates
- Check TB test expiration dates for all staff
- CPR/First Aid certification check — who is expiring in the next 6 months?
- Emergency kit inventory (full 72-hour kit contents)
- Test all fire extinguishers (visual inspection — annual professional inspection is separate)
- Walk the perimeter for outdoor equipment hazards

### Annually (before your scheduled inspection)
- Complete staff file review
- Complete child file review (pull every active enrollment file)
- Fire extinguisher professional inspection (schedule 6 weeks out)
- Review and update emergency evacuation plans
- Re-post all required lobby and classroom notices
- Walkthrough with your most trusted lead teacher as a "second set of eyes"

## The Relationship with Your Licensor

Chrystal King is not the enemy. Licensing representatives want to see compliant, well-run centers — it makes their job easier too.

**Build a professional relationship:**
- Respond to her calls and emails promptly, even when it's inconvenient
- If you discover a compliance issue before an inspection, call her proactively. Self-reporting a gap and showing it's been corrected is viewed very differently than being caught off-guard.
- Send a brief thank-you after inspections — even difficult ones. *"Thank you for the thorough review — we've already addressed [x] and it made us better."*

A director who is known as responsive, honest, and compliant gets the benefit of the doubt on borderline issues. A director who is adversarial, evasive, or defensive does not.

## Common Deficiencies: Quick Reference

*The items that come up most often in Virginia childcare inspections.*

| Area | Common Gap |
|---|---|
| Staff files | Expired TB, missing CPR, incomplete application |
| Child files | Immunization records not current, missing physical |
| Ratios | Even brief out-of-ratio during transitions |
| Fire drills | Missing month, incomplete log (no evacuation time) |
| First aid kits | Missing items, kit not accessible in classroom |
| Hazardous materials | Cleaning supplies accessible to children |
| Lesson plans | Not posted or not on file |
| Medicine box | Non-authorized items present |
| Infant room | Propping observed, whiteboard not current |
| Emergency info | Not accessible in each classroom |
