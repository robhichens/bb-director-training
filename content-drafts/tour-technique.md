# Tour Technique
*Draft content — Module 2 addition (Section 2.8)*

---

## Learning Objectives

By the end of this section, you will be able to:
- Prepare the building for a tour so it sells itself before you say a word
- Lead a structured, confident tour that addresses every family concern
- Handle the most common objections without sounding defensive
- Ask for the deposit before the family walks out the door
- Execute a follow-up sequence that converts waitlisted families

---

## The Mindset Shift

A tour is not a showing. It's a conversation with someone who is trying to make one of the most important decisions of their life — who will care for their child while they're at work.

They are not shopping for a commodity. They are looking for someone they trust. Your job on the tour is to become that person.

**The family already visited your website. They know your hours and your rates.** What they came to find out is: *do I feel good about this place? Do I feel good about the people?*

Every part of the tour experience answers that question — the lobby, the classrooms, the director's eye contact, the teacher who waves from inside the room.

## Before the Tour: Pre-Tour Prep

### The Building

Walk the lobby and hallways before any scheduled tour. Specifically look for:

- Postings current and neat (licensing certificate, menus, parent rights notice — see Module 5 lobby posting list)
- Nothing on walls or doors that wasn't approved — a random flyer or child's art taped to a classroom door is a first impression problem
- Entry clean, no jackets on hooks, no clutter at the front desk
- It smells clean — you become nose-blind to your own building. Have someone check.

### Know Who's Coming

Before the tour:
- What age is the child?
- What start date are they considering?
- Did they call in, fill out a form, or get referred? Referrals close differently.

If you know the child's name before they arrive, use it during the tour. *"We actually have a spot in our Twos room that would be perfect for Emma."* That specificity signals attentiveness.

### Alert Your Teachers

Text or GroupMe your lead teachers when a tour is scheduled:
> "Tour coming through at 10 AM — 2-year-old. Quick heads up."

A teacher who knows a tour is coming will make sure their room is presentable and will naturally smile and engage when you walk through. A teacher caught off guard may be mid-discipline or distracted. One awkward classroom moment can lose an enrollment.

## The Tour Flow

### Step 1: The Greeting (2–3 minutes)

Meet the family at the door — not at your desk. Come to them.

Greet them by name. Crouch down to the child's level if they're with the family.

> *"Hi, you must be the Johnson family — I'm [name], the director. And this must be Emma! Hi, Emma."*

Small talk for 60–90 seconds before moving. Let them settle. A family that walks in stressed from traffic needs 90 seconds before they can actually absorb anything you say.

Do not start with paperwork, rates, or policy. Not yet.

### Step 2: Set the Agenda (30 seconds)

> *"I'll walk you through the whole building, show you Emma's potential classroom, and then we'll sit down and I'll answer any questions you have. Does that work?"*

This tells them the tour has a structure and ends with a conversation — which means they're not trying to ask questions while you're walking and they're half-listening.

### Step 3: The Lobby and Common Areas

Point out:
- The parent communication board (newsletter, lunch menu, event calendar)
- How check-in/check-out works (ProCare, security)
- Where to find you — "my door is always open"

One sentence on safety without making it feel alarming:
> *"Every door requires a code or a badge, so no one gets in or out without us knowing."*

### Step 4: The Target Classroom

Go to their child's age-group classroom first — not last. This is what they came to see.

**Stand at the door, don't rush in.** Let them watch for a moment before you narrate. A good classroom sells itself in 30 seconds of observation — children engaged, teacher warm and attentive, room organized.

Then narrate what they're seeing:
> *"This is our Twos room. You can see they're in small groups right now — that's intentional. We keep our Twos to eight children with two teachers, so no child is ever lost in the group."*

Specific details that matter to parents:
- **Ratio** — always state it. Parents worry about this but won't always ask.
- **The teachers** — introduce by name if possible. *"Ms. Keisha has been with us for four years."* Tenure signals stability.
- **Curriculum approach** — one sentence. *"At this age it's all play-based — they're learning through doing, not sitting."*
- **Nap/rest time** — especially for Twos and Threes. Parents always wonder about this.
- **Communication** — *"You'll get a daily report through our app, Tadpoles — photos, meals, nap times. You'll know their whole day."*

### Step 5: The Rest of the Building

Keep this moving — 10 minutes maximum for remaining classrooms, outdoor space, and kitchen.

You're showing the full environment, not conducting a second tour in each room. A sentence or two per space.

The outdoor space is underrated. Families love seeing a well-maintained playground. Point it out with genuine enthusiasm.

### Step 6: Sit Down

Return to your office or a quiet table. This is where the real conversation happens.

Open with:
> *"What questions do you have for me?"*

Not *"So what do you think?"* — that's a closed question that invites a non-committal answer. *"What questions"* invites engagement.

## Handling Common Objections

### "We're looking at a few other places."

> *"That makes total sense — this is a big decision. What's most important to you in choosing a school? I want to make sure you're comparing the right things."*

This reframes the conversation and gives you information. If they say "we care most about curriculum," you can speak to that specifically. If they say "honestly it's mostly about price," you know what you're working with.

### "Your rates are higher than [other center]."

Do not apologize for your rates. Do not bad-mouth the competition.

> *"I understand. Our rates reflect our staff — we have lower turnover than most centers in the area, which means Emma would have consistent relationships with her teachers year after year. That consistency matters a lot for this age. But I hear you on the budget piece — can I ask what you're working with?"*

If they're genuinely price-sensitive, discuss your current enrollment options — are there any openings in a less expensive age group, any payment plan options, or any DSS eligibility?

### "We need to think about it / talk it over."

This almost always means one of two things: (a) there's a concern they haven't voiced, or (b) the other decision-maker in the family isn't present.

> *"Of course — I'd expect nothing less for a decision this big. Can I ask — is there anything specific you're weighing, or anything I didn't cover that would help?"*

If they identify a concern, address it directly. If it's about the other partner not being present:

> *"Would it be helpful to schedule a second visit so [partner] can see it too? We can even do a quick virtual walk-through if that's easier."*

### "Do you have openings? We heard there's a waitlist."

> *"We do have a waitlist for some age groups, which tells you something about how families feel about this place. But let me check exactly where we stand for Emma's age — I don't want you to assume there's no path forward."*

Check your actual availability and be honest. If there's a waitlist, explain what the process looks like and what the family can do to secure a spot when one opens.

## Asking for the Deposit

This is where most directors leave enrollment on the table. They have a great tour, the family clearly loves the center, and then they say *"take your time and let me know"* — and the family walks out and gets a call from another school that evening.

When the conversation is going well and you've addressed their questions:

> *"Based on what you've seen today, would you like to go ahead and secure Emma's spot? Our enrollment deposit is [amount] and it holds her place."*

Direct. Not pushy. Treat it as the natural next step — because it is.

If they hesitate:

> *"Even if you're not 100% ready, the deposit is fully refundable if you decide within the next [X] days — it just means no one else takes her spot while you're thinking it over."*

If they truly aren't ready to decide today, set a specific follow-up:

> *"When do you think you'll have a sense of direction — end of the week? I'll reach out Thursday to see if you have any other questions."*

Then **follow up on Thursday.** The family who doesn't hear from you assumes you don't need their business.

## The $25,800 Number

Every family who walks through your door represents a potential lifetime relationship with the center.

At an average enrollment of 3 years × $8,600/year: **$25,800 per child.** Two-parent family with two children: **$51,600.**

This isn't about being transactional. It's about recognizing that how you run a tour is one of the most financially significant things you do as a director. A tour that converts is not just good for one family — it's good for your staff's job security, your ability to invest in the building, and the center's long-term health.

Treat every tour with that weight.

## Post-Tour Follow-Up Sequence

| Timing | Action |
|---|---|
| Same day (within 2 hours) | Text or email: *"Great meeting you today — here's our enrollment packet if you'd like to review it."* |
| Day 3 (if no response) | Personal call: *"Just checking in — any questions after your visit?"* |
| Day 7 (if no response) | Final follow-up: *"We have a spot available for [start date] — wanted to make sure you knew before we moved to the next family on our list."* |
| Day 14+ | Move to general waitlist contact; reallocate your follow-up time |

Three touches is the standard. More than three without engagement and you risk damaging the relationship.

## Quick Reference: Tour Checklist

**Pre-tour:**
- [ ] Lobby clean and postings current
- [ ] Alert teachers
- [ ] Know the child's name and age

**During tour:**
- [ ] Greet at the door, use family's name
- [ ] 90-second settle-in before moving
- [ ] Set the agenda
- [ ] Classroom first, ratio and teacher names mentioned
- [ ] Tadpoles communication explained
- [ ] Sit down for questions

**At close:**
- [ ] Asked what questions they have
- [ ] Addressed any objections directly
- [ ] Asked for the deposit
- [ ] If not ready: set specific follow-up date and time

**Post-tour:**
- [ ] Same-day follow-up text/email sent
- [ ] Follow-up call scheduled in calendar
